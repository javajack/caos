Every Indian business is simultaneously a vendor to some businesses and a customer to others. The same **1.51 crore active GST taxpayers** ([GST@8 Report via A2Z Taxcorp](https://a2ztaxcorp.net/gst-8-years-report-released-gstn-highlights-record-revenue-compliance-milestones/)) appear on both sides of the invoice — and the **9.92 lakh e-invoice generators** plus **81.88 lakh registered e-invoice recipients** ([same source](https://a2ztaxcorp.net/gst-8-years-report-released-gstn-highlights-record-revenue-compliance-milestones/)) are the verified subset whose B2B flow is digitally observable today.

Counterparties are the **cheapest** stakeholder class to onboard (no purchase decision, just a one-tap upload + GSTIN verification) and the **highest network-effect contributor** — every verified vendor profile becomes a free verification for every next enterprise that pays them. The [Phase 2 vendor-network flywheel](/caos/flywheel/phase-2-vendor-network/) is built on this property.

<Aside type="tip" title="Why counterparties are stakeholders, not just background">
explicitly elevates vendors (S11) and customers (S12) to stakeholder status. They are not buyers in Phase 1 — they are buyer's-counterparties. But by Phase 2 they become users (one-tap upload, balance confirmation reply); by Phase 3 some convert to direct paying users (small business CAOS SKU); by Phase 4 they are nodes in the consent-mediated fact graph that lenders pay to query.
</Aside>

The **9.92 lakh e-invoice generators** are the most digitally engaged sub-pool — every B2B invoice they issue gets an IRN (Invoice Reference Number) from one of the 6 authorised Invoice Registration Portals (IRPs) and auto-flows to GSTR-1, GSTR-2A, GSTR-2B. That digital footprint is what makes vendor-side verification cheap: the GSTN already certifies the GSTIN, the IRN proves the invoice exists, the e-way bill confirms movement.

## The customer universe (S12) — mirror side

The customer side is the **same 1.51 cr GST taxpayer pool**, viewed from the other side of the invoice. There is no separate "customer registry" — every entity that buys from a vendor becomes their customer for that transaction.

For B2B transactions, the customer (S12) is also a registered taxpayer (GSTIN). For B2C, the customer is an individual or unregistered entity (the "small B" of B2C in GST parlance). The 81.88 lakh e-invoice recipients are essentially the B2B customer universe that received a digitally-issued invoice — the same set as the vendor universe minus exporters and SEZ outliers.

The customer-side workflows are the **mirror image** of the vendor-side workflows:

| Vendor-side workflow (S11) | Customer-side workflow (S12) |
|---|---|
| Issue tax invoice / e-invoice | Receive tax invoice / e-invoice |
| File GSTR-1 (auto-flows to customer's 2A/2B) | Receive auto-populated GSTR-2B from GSTN |
| Issue credit note / debit note | Receive credit note / debit note |
| Respond to balance confirmation | Issue balance confirmation request |
| Receive payment + TDS deduction | Make payment + deduct TDS |
| Send payment receipt | Receive payment receipt |
| File GSTR-3B with own ITC + outward tax | File GSTR-3B with own ITC (using vendor's invoice as evidence) |

The mirror property is what makes the [Phase 2 flywheel](/caos/flywheel/phase-2-vendor-network/) network-effect-rich: every transaction has two sides, and CAOS-mediating one side automatically gives partial visibility into the other.

## The two regulatory wedges that force vendor-master discipline

Two specific provisions in current Indian law make accurate vendor-master maintenance a **legally compelled budget line** for every Indian mid-market and large company. Together they explain why vendor onboarding + KYC + MSME validation is the single most-budgeted finance-team use case in Y2 of [the composite flywheel](/caos/flywheel/composite/).

### Sec 194Q — TDS on purchases (active)

Buyer of goods with turnover > ₹10 cr in preceding FY must deduct **0.1% TDS** when annual purchase from any single seller exceeds ₹50 lakh. The buyer must:

- Maintain seller-wise running purchase totals across the year
- Track the ₹50 lakh threshold per seller
- Deduct 0.1% from invoice value above the threshold
- Deposit TDS by 7th of next month
- File quarterly TDS return (Form 26Q with section code 194Q)

ERPs do not natively compute the ₹50L threshold per counterparty + reconcile against deducted amount. ([ClearTax — Section 194Q overview](https://cleartax.in/s/an-overview-on-section-194q-of-the-income-tax-act-1961-ita))

### Sec 43B(h) — MSME 45-day payment clock (active)

Inserted via Finance Act 2023, effective AY 2024-25. **Payments to Micro and Small enterprises beyond 45 days are disallowed as expense in the IT computation** until actually paid. Implications:

- Buyer must classify every vendor's MSME status (Micro / Small / Medium / Non-MSME)
- Track invoice ageing per MSME-Small vendor against the 45-day clock
- Reverse-out the deduction in IT computation if unpaid past 45 days at year-end
- Re-claim the deduction in the year of actual payment

The classification source is the Udyam registry, but the 7.83 crore Udyam database is not natively integrated into ERPs. SAP has a flag but no watchlist; Tally has nothing structured; Oracle has it as a custom field. **No incumbent owns the vendor-master + MSME-classification + 45-day-watchlist combination.** This is the wedge for the AP Manager (F5) and the Direct Tax Lead (F4).

### Sec 206C(1H) — REMOVED

<Aside type="caution" title="Budget 2025 removed Sec 206C(1H)">
File 13 use case #8 referenced Sec 206C(1H) (TCS on sales — seller-side mirror of 194Q for cross-charging discipline). **This was REMOVED from 1 April 2025 in the Union Budget 2025.** ([TaxGuru — TCS removed FAQ](https://taxguru.in/income-tax/tcs-sale-goods-removed-april-1-2025-faqs.html))

Implication for the product: 206C(1H) is no longer a use case in the Top 25 ranking. Sec 194Q remains active and is the single TDS provision that requires vendor-master + counterparty-level accumulation.
</Aside>

## The vendor onboarding pain (no incumbent owns it)

Today's typical vendor onboarding workflow at a mid-market Indian company (₹50-500 Cr revenue):

1. **Procurement requests new vendor** → emails Vendor Onboarding Form (PDF) to vendor
2. **Vendor fills the form** → emails back with GSTIN copy, PAN copy, cancelled cheque, MSME certificate (if applicable), bank statement
3. **Procurement-Finance reviews** → manually validates GSTIN on GSTN portal, PAN on income tax portal, MSME on Udyam portal, bank IFSC on bank portal
4. **Compliance / legal does sanctions check** → manual check against UN, OFAC, MHA published lists
5. **AP creates vendor master in ERP** → re-keys GSTIN, PAN, bank, address, GL mapping
6. **Procurement issues PO** → vendor finally gets paid

This typically takes **5-15 business days** with high data-entry error rate. CAOS's wedge is collapsing the loop to **30 minutes** with a single self-service link sent to the vendor — vendor uploads one set of docs, CAOS validates against all four portals, generates a vendor master entry with confidence scores, posts to the ERP via integration.

The same flow generates the [counterparty master](/caos/product/atomic-primitives/) entry (P2 primitive) that powers:

- GSTR-2B reconciliation against purchase register (use case #1, F3 GST Lead)
- Sec 194Q running threshold tracking (use case #8, F4 Direct Tax Lead)
- MSME 45-day watchlist (use case #3, F5 AP Manager + F4 Direct Tax Lead)
- Year-end balance confirmation campaign (use case #7, F6 AR Manager + F7 Audit Liaison)
- Vendor-side credit underwriting (Phase 4, S14 lender side)

A single vendor onboarding flow feeds five downstream finance-team use cases. That is the [primitive-reuse principle](/caos/product/atomic-primitives/) operating at the data-model level.
