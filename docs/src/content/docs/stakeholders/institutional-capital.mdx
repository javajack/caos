Institutional capital — banks, NBFCs, lenders, investors — sits at the top of the [composite flywheel](/caos/flywheel/composite/). They do not buy compliance software directly. They **consume the data the rest of the network produces** to underwrite loans, monitor covenants, do diligence, and price risk.

The Account Aggregator framework has made this consumption legally observable and consent-mediated. The institutional capital pool is therefore the **Phase 4 buyer side of the fact graph** — they pay CAOS for graph access (subscription on the consumer side of the consent infrastructure), not for being a vendor.

<Aside type="tip" title="Why &quot;not just being a vendor&quot;">
The lender-as-stakeholder reading does not treat banks as buyers of CAOS SaaS. It treats banks as buyers of consent-mediated data flowing through CAOS-onboarded entities. Phase 4 monetisation is structurally similar to Plaid in the US — entity-side users get the platform free or cheap; institutional consumers pay for graph access; the consent flow is the legal substrate.)
</Aside>

## AA composition — lending dominates the consent flow

The AA framework's consent flows are dominated by lending use cases. Roughly **74% of AA consents** are tied to retail and MSME lending decisions (Sahamati's reported share of lending vs other use cases varies; the dominant share is lending). The remaining 26% covers wealth management onboarding, insurance underwriting, and credit-monitoring services.

| AA consent driver | Share of consents | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Retail + MSME lending** | ~74% | Phase 4 monetisation should focus on lender-side subscription to MSME / SME entity graph |
| Wealth management onboarding | ~10-15% | Adjacent — AA infrastructure commonality with PMS / mutual fund onboarding |
| Insurance underwriting | ~5-10% | Health insurance + commercial underwriting use cases |
| Credit monitoring (consumer + commercial) | ~5-10% | Continuous data refresh post-loan-disbursal |

## DigiLocker — the consumer-side trust adjacency

DigiLocker is the consumer-side analogue to AA (document storage and verified-issuer access). Its scale gives a useful read on Indian comfort with consent-driven sharing:

| Metric | Value | As of | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| **DigiLocker registered users** | **62 cr+ (620 million)** | May 2026 | [DigiLocker home](https://www.digilocker.gov.in/) |
| **DigiLocker documents issued** | **950 cr+ (9.5 billion)** | May 2026 | [DigiLocker home](https://www.digilocker.gov.in/) |
| Active issuers | 1,936 | Mar 2025 | [NeGD-published research](https://negd.gov.in/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/Charru-Malhotra-DIgilocker-the-digital-Vault-of-India.pdf) |
| Active requesters | 2,407 | Mar 2025 | [NeGD-published research](https://negd.gov.in/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/Charru-Malhotra-DIgilocker-the-digital-Vault-of-India.pdf) |

DigiLocker's 62 cr users + 950 cr docs issued is the strongest signal that **consumer-side comfort with consent-driven document sharing already exists at scale** — the AA framework's consent flow leverages the same primitive comfort.
