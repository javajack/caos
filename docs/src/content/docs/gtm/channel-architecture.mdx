The channel question is not "which channel produces the most leads?" — it is "which channel-buyer-SKU combination compounds, and which one only converts the lead in front of it?" In Indian B2B SaaS for compliance, the difference is decisive. CA-referral compounds because every successful referral makes the referrer a stronger advocate; field sales does not compound, because every closed deal still requires the same SDR-to-AE handoff for the next one. The channel architecture below biases hard toward compounding channels in the early phases, with non-compounding channels (field sales, SI partnerships) reserved for the deal sizes where they pay back inside one quarter.

The architecture has eight channels. Each one is anchored to a verified data point (the GTM updates pass that replaced estimates with measured numbers).

<Aside type="tip" title="Compounding vs non-compounding channels">
Compounding channels (CA-referral, ICAI branch, Tally Master partner, audit-firm partnership) get cheaper per-deal as the channel matures. Non-compounding channels (field sales, SI, paid SEO) cost the same per-deal indefinitely. The Phase 1-2 channel mix is 70%+ compounding; the Phase 3-4 mix shifts to ~50/50 because enterprise deals justify the non-compounding spend.
</Aside>

## The four hard rules

The channel architecture is governed by four rules that have to hold even when individual channels look attractive in the short term:

1. **CA wedge first.** Until 1,000 paying CA firms or ₹3 cr ARR (whichever earlier), no more than 20% of channel investment on enterprise-only channels (field sales, SI partnerships, Big-4 dedicated reps). All shared channels (CA-referral, Tally partner, ICAI branch) count as CA channels.

2. **Don't sell direct to a CA's existing client without the CA knowing.** Build a CA-referral compensation engine (15-20% rev-share). Make CAs feel they own the customer relationship — the moment a CA suspects CAOS is poaching, the entire CA-referral channel collapses.

3. **Don't accept enterprise RFPs that require &lt;60-day procurement turnaround.** Walk away from procurement processes designed around incumbents (the "Tally vendor is the default; you can compete only if you replicate Tally's terms" trap).

4. **Pricing must be transparent.** Publish all SKUs and add-on prices on the website. Don't run an "enterprise: contact sales" model — Indian buyers hate it, and it kills PLG conversion at the lower-mid band.

## Channel investment per phase

The cumulative channel-investment budget by phase, with the dominant channel called out:

| Phase | Year | Channel investment band | Dominant channel |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 | Y1 | ₹50 lakh-1.5 cr | CA-referral comp engine + ICAI branch MOU + Tally Master partner pilot |
| Phase 2 | Y2 | ₹2-4 cr | CA-referral scaling + first 2-3 field sales reps + content/SEO |
| Phase 3 | Y3 | ₹6-12 cr | Mid-tier audit firm partnership + first Big-6 lighthouse + 4-6 field sales |
| Phase 4 | Y4 | ₹15-25 cr | DPI ecosystem partnerships + lender partnerships + 8-12 field sales + first SI partnership |
| Phase 5 | Y5 | ₹30-50 cr | Full channel mix + brand + institutional consumer side |

These are rough bands; actual spend depends on channel-mix performance per quarter. The discipline is the **channel-investment-to-ARR ratio**: never let any single channel exceed 40% of new logos in any quarter (single-channel risk), never let non-compounding channels exceed 50% of total spend (CAC-trap risk).
